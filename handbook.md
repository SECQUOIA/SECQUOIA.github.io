This handbook is for PhD students in SECQUOIA — those who are just arriving, those in the thick of it, and those close to finishing. It is meant to be honest, not just encouraging. It describes how we actually work, what we expect from each other, and how to navigate the harder parts.

---

## What is a PhD, really?

A PhD is a training in original research — not a series of courses or a stack of papers. By the end, you should be able to identify a problem worth solving, develop a method to address it, and communicate what you found to a community that cares. The degree is a byproduct of that process.

At SECQUOIA, a PhD also means becoming someone who can contribute independently to the fields of optimization, quantum computing, and process systems engineering. That is the real standard.

---

## David's role and yours

David's job is to secure the funding that makes your work possible and to inspire and frame ideas at the level of research direction and contribution. He is not here to manage your daily schedule or tell you what to do each morning.

Your job is to become an independent researcher. That means owning your project, driving it forward when things are unclear, and asking for help when you are stuck — not after weeks of being stuck.

This division is intentional. Dependence does not prepare you for what comes after SECQUOIA.

---

## Your first 30 days

Before you write a single line of code or prove a single theorem, test your idea in the worst case. What are the corner cases? What would have to be true for this approach to fail? What assumptions is the whole thing resting on?

This is not pessimism — it is how you find out early whether the idea has legs. A week spent stress-testing a hypothesis before building anything can save months of work later.

---

## The weekly rhythm

A typical week in SECQUOIA includes:

- **Group meeting** — where the whole group presents, discusses ideas, and holds each other accountable to progress
- **Weekly 1-on-1 with David** — your dedicated time to discuss your project, blockers, and direction
- **TAing** — teaching is part of training, not a distraction from research
- **Advising an undergraduate** — mentoring is part of your role in the community
- **PSE seminar** — staying connected to the broader field
- **Classes** (when applicable, especially in your first two years)
- **Research** — the rest is yours to drive

When you and David are both available and on campus, meetings are in person. Two days per week in the office is the minimum; being present in the lab matters for the group and for your own development.

---

## Communication

Be direct. There is no single right way to tell David or your colleagues that you are struggling — personally, academically, or with your project. The wrong way is not saying anything at all.

Struggling in silence costs you time you cannot get back, and it costs the group the chance to help. No one is expected to have everything figured out. Everyone is expected to communicate.

---

## Research: how we approach it

### Testing ideas

Start from the hardest case, not the easiest. If your method cannot handle the corner case, you want to know that before you have built everything around it.

### Dead ends

If you believe a direction is not working, bring it to David. We evaluate it together over a defined period — not indefinitely — and then decide together whether to continue or change course. Neither of you has to be right; you have to figure it out together.

### Disagreements

If you think David is wrong about something, say so with arguments. Respectful disagreement is expected and welcomed. It is how the group makes better decisions.

### Tools

We use the right tool for the right task. No language, solver, or framework is sacred. Use what the problem demands.

## Authorship and credit

We follow clear conventions:

- **First author**: the person who did the most work on the paper
- **Co-first authorship**: used when more than one person made the primary contribution — noted explicitly in the paper
- **Senior and corresponding author**: David, listed last

These rules apply consistently. If you believe a contribution is not being recognized fairly, bring it up directly.

---

## Conferences and presentations

David is a strong advocate for students attending and presenting at conferences. Getting your work in front of the community — and meeting the people whose papers you read — is a core part of the training.

**Contribution requirements to attend:**

| Conference type | First-year students | All others |
|---|---|---|
| Without proceedings | 1 contribution | 2 contributions |
| With proceedings | 1 accepted paper | 1 accepted paper |

Presenting before you graduate is expected. It is part of what "contribution to the community" means in this group.

## The valley of shit

At some point in your PhD — usually somewhere in the middle — you will hit a period where the work is hard, the end feels impossibly far away, and you are not sure you are capable of finishing. This period has a name: [the valley of shit](https://thesiswhisperer.com/2012/05/08/the-valley-of-shit/), a term coined by Inger Mewburn at *The Thesis Whisperer*.

It is real. It happens to nearly everyone. It does not mean you should quit.

What helps:
- **Remember the big picture.** Some parts of a PhD are genuinely unglamorous — debugging code you didn't write, rerunning experiments, rewriting the same section five times. Within the larger arc of the work, those pieces add up to something that matters. Keep that picture in view.
- **Talk to your colleagues.** They are probably in a similar place, or they have been. The lab exists as a community partly for this reason.
- **Tell David.** There is no wrong time to have that conversation, only a wrong choice not to have it.

## Graduating from SECQUOIA

Purdue has formal requirements for the PhD. This section is about David's standard.

A SECQUOIA PhD means you have:

- Made a genuine, communicable contribution to the field — something new that the community can build on
- Published and presented that work so others know it exists and can use it
- Built enough of a record — papers, software, presentations, collaborations — to thrive wherever you want to go after graduation

The goal is not clearing a minimum bar. It is leaving ready.

---

## Industry offers and other opportunities

When an opportunity arises during your PhD — an industry offer, a fellowship, a postdoc, a collaboration — bring it to David. The right answer depends entirely on who you are, where you are in the program, and where you want to go. There is no standing rule that a given opportunity is automatically the right or wrong move.

SECQUOIA trains people for many things: faculty positions, national labs, industry, startups, policy. All of those paths are supported.

---

## Vacations

Take them. Self-request your vacation and it will be approved. Rest is not a reward for productivity — it is what makes sustained, rigorous work possible.

---

## External collaborations

When you work with people outside the lab — at other universities, national labs, or in industry — you are representing SECQUOIA. David should be in the loop: on the collaboration, the outputs, and how credit is being shared. This is not oversight; it is how the group works as a unit.
